The Case for Slow Reading

By Jordan Avery

We have built an entire economy around speed. Headlines are engineered to be skimmed, articles are optimized to be scrolled, and the reward for finishing anything is simply the next thing. Yet something is lost when reading becomes a race.

Slow reading is not about reading less. It is about reading with attention. When you give a single piece of writing your full focus, you begin to notice the structure of an argument, the texture of a sentence, and the spaces where the author chose to pause.

Researchers who study attention have found that comprehension and retention both improve when readers are free from the pull of notifications and adjacent links. The page, stripped of its surrounding noise, becomes a place to think rather than a place to react.

This is the quiet promise of saving an article for later. The act of setting something aside is a small commitment to return with intention, often in a calmer moment, when the words have room to land.

So the next time you find something worth reading, consider not reading it right now. Save it. Come back to it on a walk, on a train, or in the last quiet hour of the day. Give it the attention it was written to deserve.
